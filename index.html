Core concepts in 3D genomics

Think of chromosomes as polymers in a nucleus. Hi-C gives you a *population-average* proximity matrix, not a single structure.

mental model
Matrix

Contact map

A square matrix where cell (i, j) reflects how often loci i and j were close enough to ligate. The diagonal is strongest because nearby loci contact more often.

Scale

Multi-resolution exploration

Large-scale patterns (compartments) appear at coarse resolution; finer resolution reveals domains and loops. Zooming is the whole game.

Features

Compartments, domains, loops

Compartments look like plaid/checker textures; domains are squares/triangles along the diagonal; loops are bright focal dots off the diagonal.

What Hi-C measures (high-level)

A biochemical proximity assay → sequencing reads → pairs of genomic coordinates → binned into a matrix.

overview
1

Crosslink + cut

Fix chromatin interactions, digest DNA (restriction enzyme), leaving fragments that reflect 3D proximity.

2

Fill + ligate

Fill ends and ligate so fragments that were near each other become joined (a chimeric junction).

3

Sequence + map

Paired-end reads are mapped to the genome, producing pairs of loci (i, j) for each contact.

4

Bin + normalize

Bin contacts at a chosen resolution and optionally normalize to reduce technical biases before visualization.

Tip: Use resolution to match your question (compartments vs loops). Use normalization to compare regions fairly. Remember distance decay dominates near the diagonal.

How to read a contact map (fast intuition)

Patterns map to biology, but also to scale. The same region can look “featureless” at one zoom and rich at another.

interpretation

Diagonal + decay

Contacts drop with genomic distance. Strong diagonal is expected; compare like-with-like distances when judging enrichment.

Domains (TAD-like)

Squares/triangles along the diagonal suggest neighborhoods of elevated internal contact frequency.

Loops

Bright puncta away from the diagonal often correspond to specific long-range interactions (e.g., CTCF/cohesin-mediated).

★
A practical viewing loop
Start broad (Mb) to spot compartments and major rearrangements → zoom to 100–50 kb for domains → zoom to 10–5 kb (if coverage supports) for loops. Toggle normalization when comparing across regions or samples.

Workflow mindset

What you can do depends on coverage and resolution. Always match analysis scale to sequencing depth and biological question.

practice

Choose resolution honestly

If bins are too small for your depth, the map looks noisy and loops are unreliable. Coarser bins can be more informative.

Compare with intention

When comparing conditions, keep preprocessing consistent and lean on normalization / distance-aware expectations.

Annotate what you see

Use tracks (genes, ChIP-seq, peaks) and feature calls (loops/domains) to connect patterns to mechanism.
